Yoga Philosophy

Yoga is not a set of exercises. It is a complete system for understanding the self, the world, and the relationship between them.

The foundation

What is yoga?

The Sanskrit word yoga comes from the root yuj — to yoke, to unite. Yoga is union.

In the modern West, yoga is frequently understood as a physical practice — a form of Indian gymnastics, in the memorable phrase. This is a significant misunderstanding. The physical postures (asana) constitute one limb of Patanjali's eightfold path, which extends from ethical living through to the direct experience of undivided consciousness (Samadhi).

Yoga was actually conceived to address existential questions: Where did we come from? Where are we going? What is the purpose of existence? Who am I? These are not philosophical puzzles to be solved theoretically — they are living inquiries to be explored through practice, breath, and direct experience.

"An ounce of practice is better than tons of theory."

— Swami Sivananda

At Ajanta Yoga Academy, we approach yoga as an empirical science. The laboratory is one's own being. The methods are standardised and repeatable. The results — greater clarity, equanimity, and a felt sense of wholeness — are reliably available to those who practice sincerely.

Patanjali's Ashtanga

The Eight Limbs of Yoga

A complete map of the inner journey, from ethical foundations to the direct experience of unity.

Limbs 1–2

Yama & Niyama

Ethical living & personal discipline. The five Yamas (non-harming, truthfulness, non-stealing, continence, non-grasping) and five Niyamas (purity, contentment, discipline, self-study, devotion) form the moral and psychological foundation of the entire path.

Limb 3

Asana

Posture. Patanjali describes asana simply as "steady and comfortable." Its purpose is to prepare the body for the stillness of meditation — not to achieve aesthetic or athletic goals. In our adaptive practice, this limb adapts to the body available.

Limb 4

Pranayama

Breath regulation. Prana is the life force itself — more subtle than air or oxygen. Pranayama works with breathing to influence the flow of prana through the energy channels (nadis), ultimately affecting consciousness itself.

Limb 5

Pratyahara

Sensory withdrawal. Turning attention inward. The classic metaphor is the turtle drawing in its limbs — a voluntary disconnection from external stimulation that opens the door to the three inner limbs.

Limbs 6–7

Dharana & Dhyana

Concentration & meditation. Dharana is single-pointed focus — the voluntary binding of attention to one object. When this sustained effortlessly, it becomes Dhyana: meditation, an unbroken flow of awareness toward the same point.

Limb 8

Samadhi

Absorption. The dissolution of the distinction between the meditator, the act of meditation, and the object of meditation. The individual I-consciousness merges with the Supreme Consciousness — as a crystal of salt dissolves in water.

A core teaching

Unity & Polarity

The entire manifest universe is characterised by polarity. Yoga is the practice of finding unity within that duality.

Hatha Yoga — ha (sun) and tha (moon) — is named after the fundamental polarity of existence. Active and passive, giving and receiving, solar and lunar, masculine and feminine. Every pair of opposites in the universe is reflected in the structure of the human being.

The goal of yoga is not to eliminate these polarities but to balance them — and, in that balance, to touch the unity that underlies all duality. This is why the Bhagavad Gita speaks of moderation in all things: neither too much nor too little, neither this extreme nor that.

☀️

Ha — Solar

Active, expansive, giving, masculine, yang. The principle of movement and outward expression.

🌙

Tha — Lunar

Passive, receptive, feminine, yin. The principle of stillness, receptivity, and inward turning.

"All techniques of Tantra and Hatha Yoga have one thing in common: their ultimate purpose. They aim to balance polarity in order to transcend the dual nature of manifestation. Their supreme goal is Oneness."

— Classical Hatha Yoga Teaching
Key terms

A philosophical vocabulary

Core Sanskrit concepts explored in our courses and articles.

Prana

Vital life force. The animating energy that exists in all things, more subtle than breath itself.

Ahimsa

Non-harming. The first and most foundational ethical principle — toward others, and toward oneself.

Sthira & Sukha

Steadiness and ease. The twin qualities of right practice — firm without struggle, comfortable without collapse.

Chitta

The fluctuating mind. Yoga Sutra I.2: "Yoga is the cessation of the movements of the mind."

Svādhyāya

Self-study. One of the Niyamas — the ongoing practice of honest inner inquiry and engagement with wisdom texts.

Samadhi

Absorption. The state in which subject and object dissolve and only pure awareness remains — the aim of the entire path.
