What does "union" really mean in yoga?

The Sanskrit root yuj — to yoke — points toward something far more radical than flexibility or fitness. An exploration of what yoga as union actually means.

10 min read · Philosophy

The word yoga appears so frequently in contemporary culture that its meaning has almost dissolved through overuse. Yoga classes, yoga mats, yoga retreats. But what did the ancient teachers of India actually mean when they used this word?

The Sanskrit root yuj means to yoke — in the sense of harnessing a horse to a cart, or joining two things together that were previously apart. Yoga is union. But union of what?

In different philosophical systems the answer varies. In Advaita Vedanta, it is the union of the individual self (Atman) with the universal self (Brahman). In Kashmiri Shaivism, it is the union of Shakti (energy, the dynamic feminine) with Shiva (consciousness, the stable ground). In the Samkhya framework underlying Patanjali's Yoga Sutras, it is the recognition that Purusha (pure consciousness) and Prakriti (nature, matter) were never truly separate...
