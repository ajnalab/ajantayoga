Foundations

Yoga is not a single system or fixed doctrine. It is a vast and evolving body of knowledge developed through centuries of practice, reflection, philosophy, ritual, and lived experience. The foundations section offers an introduction to the historical roots of yoga, its philosophical frameworks, and the central ideas that shaped different yogic traditions.

Through guided talks, readings, and discussion, we explore the origin and evolution of yoga across diverse schools and cultural contexts, including Vedic traditions, Upanishadic inquiry, classical yoga, Buddhist and Jain influences, Tantric developments, Hatha Yoga, and modern global interpretations.

Historical Evolution

  • Early ascetic and contemplative traditions
  • The Vedas and Upanishads
  • The Bhagavad Gita and paths of yoga
  • Patanjali’s Yoga Sutras
  • Tantra and Hatha Yoga traditions
  • Yoga in the modern world

Core Philosophical Themes

  • Mind, awareness, and perception
  • Suffering and liberation
  • Breath, energy, and embodiment
  • Ethics and self-discipline
  • Meditation and concentration
  • Relationship between self and world

Ancient Texts & Traditions

This series introduces major concepts found in ancient yogic literature and traces how they evolved across different traditions. Each module focuses on historical context, symbolic language, cultural influences, and practical relevance for contemporary practitioners.

The Upanishads

Exploration of consciousness, meditation, self-inquiry, and the relationship between the individual and the universal.

The Bhagavad Gita

Study of Karma Yoga, Bhakti Yoga, Jnana Yoga, duty, action, devotion, and inner balance.

Yoga Sutras of Patanjali

Examination of concentration, meditation, ethics, states of mind, and the classical eight-limbed system of yoga.

Hatha Yoga Texts

Introduction to the Hatha Yoga Pradipika, Gheranda Samhita, and other texts focused on body, breath, energetic systems, and transformative practice.

Tantric Perspectives

Understanding subtle body concepts, symbolic ritual, mantra, visualization, and the integration of embodied spirituality.

Practice

Practice sessions combine movement, breath, listening, and reflection. During classes we engage with foundational theory and guided talks while simultaneously exploring asanas, pranayamas, and meditative awareness.

The emphasis is not performance, but observation, stability, breath awareness, and gradual integration. Introductory practices are suitable for beginners while also offering depth for continuing students.

Introductory Asanas

  • Tadasana (Mountain Pose)
  • Balasana (Child’s Pose)
  • Bhujangasana (Cobra Pose)
  • Adho Mukha Svanasana (Downward Dog)
  • Virabhadrasana I & II (Warrior Poses)
  • Trikonasana (Triangle Pose)
  • Paschimottanasana (Seated Forward Fold)
  • Savasana (Resting Pose)

Introductory Pranayamas

  • Natural diaphragmatic breathing
  • Ujjayi breath awareness
  • Nadi Shodhana (alternate nostril breathing)
  • Bhramari (humming breath)
  • Viloma breathing
  • Foundations of breath retention
  • Breath observation and regulation

Theory & Embodied Understanding

Each practice includes explanation of alignment, breath mechanics, nervous system regulation, energetic interpretation, and traditional context. Students are encouraged to cultivate curiosity rather than perfection.

Yoga for Special Conditions

Yoga practices can be adapted to support physical recovery, emotional balance, stress regulation, and general wellbeing. This section explores accessible approaches for different conditions while emphasizing safety, observation, and individualized adaptation.

Physical Wellbeing

  • Mobility and joint health
  • Back and neck support
  • Postural awareness
  • Gentle strengthening
  • Recovery and restorative practices

Mental & Emotional Support

  • Stress reduction
  • Breath and nervous system regulation
  • Practices for anxiety management
  • Grounding and concentration
  • Relaxation and sleep support

General Health Improvement

  • Daily movement routines
  • Breath awareness habits
  • Mindfulness in daily life
  • Balance and coordination
  • Energy and resilience

Yoga is not presented as a substitute for medical care. Practices are educational and supportive in nature, and students are encouraged to work with qualified healthcare professionals when necessary.

Open Questions

Yoga traditions have always contained multiple viewpoints, interpretations, and methods. This section acknowledges that diversity and encourages thoughtful inquiry rather than rigid certainty.

Different Schools of Thought

Classical Yoga, Advaita Vedanta, Tantra, Buddhism, Bhakti, and modern postural yoga traditions often describe reality and practice in different ways.

Symbolic vs Literal Interpretations

Some teachings are approached metaphorically, while others are read as literal energetic or spiritual descriptions.

Tradition & Modernity

Contemporary yoga continues to evolve as it encounters modern science, psychology, medicine, and global culture.

Respectful Inquiry

Students are invited to explore different perspectives with openness, discernment, humility, and critical reflection.
